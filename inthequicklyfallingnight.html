I.


The cold, freezing wind blew against the windowpanes and they shuddered in reply. The wind lashed out at them and whipped them again. John Huntsford sat in a chair in his house, reading a letter by the pale and flickering light of the large candle that stared over haphazardly at him. His face wrinkled up in consternation and he carefully folded the letter up in his hands and stared out the window. He gasped in amazement and horror. Outside, a shuddering seemed to vibrate through the atmosphere as the cold, cruel wind shrieked and howled. John sat silent for several minutes, and then without any warning, he gasped and pounded his fist in the table as hard as he could. It rattled warningly, and he composed himself as he could, picked up the letter, opened it, and stared at the letter again and again, reading it over and over again until his eyes hurt. "Impossible! Impossible!" he muttered over and over again under his breath. The moon, nearly full, stared at him through the window mysteriously, and an owl howled in the distance. This is what the letter said.

Miss Emma Myrtle, in the twenty-third day of the ninth month of the year; in the thirty-second year of King Harolde Bergusen IV. Greetings.

My dear John, you will be very shocked when you hear this news. I really do not know how to break it to you except by saying that his honor the King, in order to enforce justice more fully (or so he says, he is very corrupt and vile) has seen fit to write a decree stating that in the thirty-first day of this month, starting at midnight as the day before, the thirtieth, dies away, his Secret Guards of the Peace have the right to go in any house, and destroy any thing that they see fit, to murder any man that they see fit to kill (Anyone, who, according to the decree, is "A disturbance to the peace," which means that they will kill anyone they like) and as you know, my dear John, that they have been doing this already; but, as is obvious; they will make their privileges very clear by murdering enough people to get "sense" and "obedience" into the citizens, and I fear, dear John, that you are in danger. For are you not third in authority in the Society of The independent Record-keepers, and that is next only to the Society of Independence on the societies and organizations hated by the guards of the peace? Oh John, I fear for your life! Please do not put yourself in danger. Even if you will not try to be careful for your own life, be careful for my sake. Remember that I love you John, and that we have agreed to get married next spring.

P.S. John, I think that the reason that the Burgusens are making this decree is because they have grown too weak and the guards of the peace have grown too powerful. They fear for their own lives and are making this decree to pacify the guards and attempt to keep themselves from being assassinated.

As always, Emma Myrtle.

"Poor Emma!" exclaimed John, "She does not realize the danger that she herself is in! A man is a thousand times more likely to be killed in the city, and she lives in the capital city. I must write her a letter at once!" He set down the letter, and got up from his chair. He walked over to the cabinet of his one-room house, and got an inkwell, in which he poured some ink out of the little glass bottle that sat on his table. He picked up his quill, dipped it in the ink, and began to write "Dear Emma..." when suddenly a loud tolling greeted his ears. It was the bell of his country church; striking twelve.

...has seen fit to write a decree stating that in the thirty first day of this month, starting at midnight as the day before, the thirtieth, dies away...

The bell had tolled midnight. It was now the thirty-first. The Guards of the Peace were out to kill any that they thought were a threat to themselves or the Bergusens. The Society of the Independent Record-Keepers was viewed as a threat by Guards of the Peace. John Huntsford belonged to this society. Emma Myrtle was in no real danger. Had he forgotten that the Guards of the peace had outposts in the forests? The guards were out to kill him.



II.


That night John's sleep was disturbed with fleeting nightmares that left him up in bed, shaking with terror. His mind leaped back and forth between fear for himself and fear for Emma. Towards the morning, however; he began to calm down, and when he finally awoke he found that it was nearly ten o' clock.

"Oh dear!" he exclaimed to himself, "It's terribly late! No time for breakfast! I must be to the meeting at ten!"

"The meeting" was the weekly meeting of the Society of the independent Record-Keepers, who were dedicated to the preservation of honest and accurate records for future civilization. They realized that their own civilization was hovering in a drunken stagger over the dusty chute of histories incinerator and wished to leave to future generations a reliable record of their own existence and advice to help prevent future civilizations from making the same mistakes that theirs had. It was only natural, then, that the Bergusen royal family and the Guards of the Peace would help even out this force for good and be selfish to end, attempting, like so many other great empires, to give an inaccurate and exaggerated record of their own greatness and in an effort to set up an eternal boasting of themselves; should try to squelch any honest attempt at record-keeping. The Society of Independent Record-Keepers understood this; and knew that the Guards of the Peace would try to destroy their records, and so they kept all the original copies of their important records in a secret vault hidden deep in a cave in the heart of Mt. Kenly. The society was actually quite new, having been established only six years before by the then thirty-year old Henry Macer.

Mr. J. Huntsford, Third in authority of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers, walked into the house of Henry Macer, who was the president of the society. Meetings were held in his home, which was located in a small village of about 50 people and was the headquarters of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. The 334th weekly meeting of the society was about to begin, and it began with the usual routine.

"The mission and oath of the Society of independent Record Keepers." said Henry Macer, and the sixteen other active members of the society repeated with him: "To preserve an honest record of our civilization, to leave our posterity with a better future, and to press forward in the preservation of a clear conscience for ourselves and better moral values for mankind."

The formality of the meeting was slightly lowered by the presence of one of Mr. Macer's two daughters, who was four years old, and sat in her father's lap, quietly sucking her thumb. Mrs. Macer and her ten-year-old daughter had gone to the local grocers to buy some food; while the younger daughter had insisted to "stay with daddy." The meeting continued and the first agenda was presented. Nothing of great importance was to be discussed, only the presentation of two new members. Mr. Macer was quite well-to-do, and he lived in a large red two-story Victorian style house with over a dozen rooms and plenty of windows. In a village that consisted mainly of farmland and where the houses were huts, this house quite stood out. Mr. Macer was known as a kind and benevolent man to the villagers, however, who often gave out money to the poor and who was willing to pay to help the youth of the village enter college, and there existed no ill feeling between him and the villagers, despite the class difference. He was now reading off the last of the planned agenda. There had been little discussion; the agenda was simple, and they were ending early. The tone was one of contentment. Everything had been going as planned and they were quite happy about the proceedings. Mr. Macer had even said: "I wonder if there is still hope for our country. Perhaps it is not as bad as we think." John Huntsford hated to break the happy mood, but he knew that he had to say something. He decided to wait no longer.

"It is not as bad as you think." he stumbled out, "it is worse!"

The talking ceased. All men stared at John. "What is this you say?" said Mr. Macer.

"I have just received a letter." began John, and then paused for a little, "from Emma Myrtle. We are, as you know, engaged, or at least we plan to get married. Her parents are dead, and she lives with her aunt, who says that she will not allow her niece to marry a member of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. They live in a three-story high rise in the city that is shared by several different families. It is only several hundred feet from the entrance to the royal palace. The King has just issued a decree..."

When he had finished, everyone was silent. There was nothing to be said. The meeting was dismissed. Little Mary had left to go to her mother, who had come back from the store. John stayed behind to talk with Mr. Macer and Samuel Wilkins, President and Vice president of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers.

"I have to tell you something very important that has been weighing on my mind." said Mr. Macer. "It is not unlikely that one of us will be killed. Obviously we will be very careful to always stay in safe places and perhaps have a weapon of self-defense at hand, but we can never eliminate that possibility to impossible. What I want to say, is that you two and myself are the only ones that I trust with the knowledge of the exact location of our secret vault. If I die, you will have all the responsibilities as president of this society, Samuel Wilkins." He turned and looked Mr. Wilkins in the eye. "Do you feel like you can accept this responsibility? It is a heavy one. You may let other people into the knowledge of the secret vault, but mind you; the possibility that one of our members is a spy or has joined the other side; that he is a traitor, grows higher and higher every minute. The people that I would have in mind that I trust fully? I don't know. Ron Quely seems to be very trustworthy, but he has not been in the society for very long. Only choose those with simple honesty. Know them well before you choose them. We have enough records in the vault to give clarity and meaning to the future. Remember. You have little to win; you have everything to lose. And," he said, turning to John, "If the unthinkable should happen, and both me and Mr. Wilkins should die, would you be able to direct this work and see if you can preserve it. If not, if we all die, than at least there will be a record, a vault, hidden deep in a mountain, that will one day be found. One day, after we are all gone and our civilization is forgotten and what remains is hopelessly distorted in oral tradition; and a new and greater civilization arises, when this civilization is faced with the question of monarchy or democracy, as ours once was, someone will find a record hidden in a mountain, and they will find it and preserve their own posterity from utter ruin. I am sure that they will make mistakes, very bad ones too, but they will have a record, a warning, given from a fallen civilization speaking from the dust, and they will establish a more perfect union. This is my hope, this is my dream." He stood silent and stared out the window for a while, saying nothing. John and Samuel Wilkins stood; lost in thought.



III.

She could be very kind and gentle, but she tried to not show that side often. Unfortunately, she had nearly succeeded. And yes, you guessed it. She felt cramped in the city. (Goodness! Only cramped? It was the largest city in the known world, with over 20,000 people!) Before her parents had died she had lived in a beautiful little country house with chickens and her own pony. But these had all been sold when her parents died, mostly to cheap meat markets. But Emma was not one to complain, and she managed to bear her afflictions with a shadow of her former happiness. She had lived with her aunt for three years now, and had hated it. She left the room in a huff, and left to go back to her sewing. She, not surprisingly, owned a sewing business and made sure that Emma sewed for it too. Emma began on her sewing again, but everything she did seemed to mess up. Her hands trembled. That Agnes Hepzibah Lôddmerkürr had once been like her, that she had once been in love, was too much for her. The horrifying thought that her beloved John would be murdered and that she would turn out like her aunt was too much for her. She put down her sewing materials and left the room to go into her bedroom. She began to sob.



IV.


John loved to go on walks, and today was not an exception. He did not feel nearly as apprehensive about his imminent death as was the woman who he strongly wished to become his aunt (despite his dislike for her which fell nothing short of hatred) and when the shock of the decree and what had to him then had seemed to be apprehensively prophetic words which President Macer had said yesterday had worn off, he found that he cared little for what a fat king fifty miles away had said. He, for one, had never seen one Guard of the Peace except on a visit to the royal palace fifteen years ago as a child. His mother had been with him and they admired the beautiful artwork and furniture in the king's house. On the way out, he had asked what the darkly dressed man holding a sword was. His mother had answered with what, looking back, had not been a very pleasant tone of voice that they were the Guards of the Peace. Very little of the populace really liked the Guards of the Peace; and if one mentioned them to someone, they would always elicited a frown. John Huntsford thought pleasantly on his parents. They were both still alive, and lived about fifteen miles away in a large town of about 5,000. He had just remembered that he had agreed to visit them tomorrow! Imagine if he had forgotten! He would have been so embarrassed.

His thoughts were interrupted by a loud birdsong and looked about him. Where was he? Than he remembered. He had not walked very far. In fact, he had walked backwards, towards his house! He laughed. He would be glad when he had a good, sensible wife to help him maintain his common sense. He walked along the path, being careful to not get distracted and get lost in the woods. Of course, if he really did get lost, it would not be such a big deal. He could just find a large hill and look around until he could see the village. In fact, it was such a hill that the path lead to. The villagers occasionally had bonfires and festivals on it. The hill had a clearing at the top, and such stumps had been put there by the villagers as would serve as seats to sit around the bonfire during a festival. It was not a very large hill, only about 20 feet above the surrounding forest, but then, it was the largest around for over a mile. Most of the forest was on level ground, but a few random hills jutted out every so often from the surrounding forest. The nearer you got to the distant mountains, the more and larger hills there were until they joined the mountains themselves. No one except a very few ever went to the mountains. John had been there twice with Mr. Macer to deliver records to the secret vault. It was quite interesting that while he was only 24, he was third in authority in the Society of Independent Record-Keepers while several men twice his age were not trusted to know the same things as he knew. He had gone to college for four years, from when he was 18 and 22 and during his summer vacation one year he had become very interested in the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. He thought that Mr. Macer was a positive genius and admired his ideas. He had joined the society that same summer that he had found out about it (he was nineteen, the minimum joining age). His parents were somewhat doubtful about his ardent admiration of the society, but were glad that their son kept good company; the society's members were good, respectable people of the sort that they would want their son to look up to, and at the core of the society's mission was the preservation of traditional moral values. After he left college, he became the society's secretary, the only paid job in the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. And when William Bialey, who was 58 and the vice president if the Society, had suddenly died of what was probably a heart attack, Samuel Wilkins had replaced him as vice president, and John's simple honesty and wholehearted industry had impressed Mr. Macer and he was appointed to third in command. He stayed the secretary and also took on that job. The members (there were over 50 at the time, and now there were hundreds, with half a dozen offices across the country, of which the home of Mr. Macer was the headquarters.) each paid a small fee which went to the secretary of the society; which was a full time job because he had scores of records to categorize and organize along with assignments to visit the other offices and meetingplaces to make sure that everything was in order. At only 24, and having had this job since he was 22, he stood out from the much older members who were not entrusted to jobs and secrets nearly as important as the ones that he was entrusted to. But John was very good at his job and it paid well. John did not know what to do with most of his money and since he had earned it; he had no interest in squandering it away, so he sent over two thirds of it back to his parent's town to put in savings.

His parents lived close to the nearest bank. They were certainly not rich, and they were certainly not poor. They lived in a modest house in the town of Rëighmuller, and John was the oldest of the five living children. John, the oldest, was, of course, 24, and next in line were two sisters; Elisabeth (21) and Ester (18). Elisabeth was married and lived in the capital city with her husband, who was going to college. Harry (Henry) was 15 and his younger brother James was 13. Several other children had died in infancy or early childhood.

John had now reached the top of the hill. With the stumps and a few old rusty firepokers, it looked quite rustic. John loved this place. It's quiet stillness attracted him. The hill had steep sides and the top was quite level. It was also almost exactly at the same level as the trees and was about fifty or so feet in diameter. Several smaller hills of similar height were also in the forest around this area. Legend had it that the hills had been made by an ancient civilization that had now been forgotten and had served some old, now unknown purpose. The other hills were all about 17 or 18 feet off the ground and viewed form a map, seemed to form a kind of cluster about this hill. This hill was about two or three feet higher than all the other hills. The meaning of these hills would probably never be known, and the civilization that built them would be forever shrouded in the folds of legend and myth. The hills were a perfect place to just sit down and meditate. It seemed as if this were a small island level with a sea of green, out of which the mountains rose in the distance and in the other direction there was an everlasting waterfall, like the end of the world, where, according to legend (and quite against the theory of a round earth that he learned in college had been proven by geometry and trigonometry) the sea dropped off in an endless chasm. Only some of the more remote farmers and peasants actually believed the theory of a flat earth, but it was the subject of many fairytales. (Peranthura falling down the abyss only to be rescued by Prince Revonion, who throws the rope down from his boat to save her; the man who formed wings out of feathers and flew out of the dungeon where he had been unjustly condemned and who flew beyond the end of the earth; et cetera, et cetera, et cetera...)

John got up from the stump that he had been sitting on. "It will soon be dark." he said, "I must be getting home!" He walked down the steep hillside and found himself under the cover of the trees again. "Oh! How I love walks! And it was such a beautiful day too. Well, I must be getting home. That is such a beautiful flower over there. If only Emma was here I would give it to her."



V.


Emma was sitting in the parlor, sewing, when the loud, deep clanging of the interrupted her. "It must almost be time for church!" she said to herself. Her aunt did not go to church, and forced Emma to sew on Sunday despite Emma's objections with loud threatenings about how if they did not sew on Sundays they might not have enough money for food (of course they had plenty) and that if they had little food she would hide it all from Emma because it was all her fault, etc. but after persistent beggings on the part of Emma, she had allowed her to go to church. Emma was not allowed to have any nice clothes, although she was made to sew plenty of them, even to wear to church, ("An extravagancy" her aunt said) but instead was made to wear dark and drab colors. However, Emma still went to church, and today was an exception. Her aunt never went to church, but instead stayed home and sewed.

Emma entered the large cathedral. Deep strains of organ music trembled through the air, and Emma fingered her one and most precious possession, the only object of hers that she had been allowed to keep after her parents had died, her Bible. She sat inconspicuously and ignored, drab and colorless, in one of the back pews. But she was more faithful than any of the pompous and grandiose nobles and courtiers, who sat in the best seats reserved for loyalty; than of the prideful and piteous throng, the thousands that pushed her aside in the aisle.

John Huntsford almost always visited his family on Saturday, and than stayed the night to attend church with them on Sunday. It was a several hour drive bu horse and carriage (the fastest available method of transportation), but in his opinion it was worth it. His parents prepared a big Sunday dinner to have after church the night before, with pies, mashed potatoes, chicken, rolls and gravy; and it was the very best country cooking. Occasionally, even Elisabeth and her husband would come down from the capital city, and all the living members of the family would be together. The Huntsfords were dedicated churchgoers, but their children had a tradition of being noisy, and the fact that church would probably soon be silent rather distressed Mrs. Huntsford, who did not chafe so much now when James mischievously reached over and yanked Harry's hair, and Harry screamed in pain, or when Harry, to get back at is brother, dropped large spiders down his brothers back and his brother shrieked loudly. Mrs. Huntsford even remembered the time when the family had been sitting in the front row of the small country church; and John, who was only nine at the time, was playing with a rubber ball, bouncing it up and down as furiously as possible, and catching it each time with his hand. He was being strongly discouraged by his parents, but they were mostly taking care of the then six and four year old sisters Ester and Elisabeth, who were screaming as loud as their lungs would allow them to and had been taken out of the chapel by their parents, leaving John alone in the pew. One wrong bounce, and the ball escaped John's hands, and at full speed, catapulted in the air, hitting the minister in the nose.

Right now John sat with his family with his hands folded together, not making one sound. He listened to the talks with interest, and he seemed to have grown since than. The minister had died, and a mew one took his place. This one was a hot headed revolter, and he often would drone on about the wrongs and injustices of the Bergusens, while everyone fell asleep. He was not nearly as well liked as the past minister, and the congregation steadily decreased as the congregation in the surrounding churches steadily increased. Another big reason for this was that he did not know the Bible well at all, and often misquoted verses in quite strange ways; which made it rather hard for people to be solemn. This time he spoke about kindness to our fellow men, and actually did quite good, but it seemed to conflict quite strangely with last week's sermon on pillaging the king's palace. But the Huntsfords tried to look past the weaknesses of their leaders, and when things got really strange, they opened up the bible and read from it.

After church, they came home and feasted on their big Sunday dinner, and Mrs. Huntsford tried to restore order the best she could when James decided to suddenly stick out his arm in front of where Henry was walking; and when Henry didn't stop in time and crashed into it, and dropping his plate on the floor, where it shattered into a thousand pieces.



VI.


Several days later Emma received a letter. It was lighthearted and told about his visit to his parents, about how well life was going, and said that he had not seen or heard of any Guards of the Peace anywhere even remotely near where he lived, and he had begun to wonder if the proclamation was simply a gust of hot air from a weak and dying dynasty as it collapsed into complete obsolescence.
