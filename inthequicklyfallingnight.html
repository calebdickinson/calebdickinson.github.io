I.


The cold, freezing wind blew against the windowpanes and they shuddered in reply. The wind lashed out at them and whipped them again. John Huntsford sat in a chair in his house, reading a letter by the pale and flickering light of the large candle that stared over haphazardly at him. His face wrinkled up in consternation and he carefully folded the letter up in his hands and stared out the window. He gasped in amazement and horror. Outside, a shuddering seemed to vibrate through the atmosphere as the cold, cruel wind shrieked and howled. John sat silent for several minutes, and then without any warning, he gasped and pounded his fist in the table as hard as he could. It rattled warningly, and he composed himself as he could, picked up the letter, opened it, and stared at the letter again and again, reading it over and over again until his eyes hurt. "Impossible! Impossible!" he muttered over and over again under his breath. The moon, nearly full, stared at him through the window mysteriously, and an owl howled in the distance. This is what the letter said.

Miss Emma Myrtle, in the twenty-third day of the ninth month of the year; in the thirty-second year of King Harolde Bergusen IV. Greetings.

My dear John, you will be very shocked when you hear this news. I really do not know how to break it to you except by saying that his honor the King, in order to enforce justice more fully (or so he says, he is very corrupt and vile) has seen fit to write a decree stating that in the thirty-first day of this month, starting at midnight as the day before, the thirtieth, dies away, his Secret Guards of the Peace have the right to go in any house, and destroy any thing that they see fit, to murder any man that they see fit to kill (Anyone, who, according to the decree, is "A disturbance to the peace," which means that they will kill anyone they like) and as you know, my dear John, that they have been doing this already; but, as is obvious; they will make their privileges very clear by murdering enough people to get "sense" and "obedience" into the citizens, and I fear, dear John, that you are in danger. For are you not third in authority in the Society of The independent Record-keepers, and that is next only to the Society of Independence on the societies and organizations hated by the guards of the peace? Oh John, I fear for your life! Please do not put yourself in danger. Even if you will not try to be careful for your own life, be careful for my sake. Remember that I love you John, and that we have agreed to get married next spring.

P.S. John, I think that the reason that the Burgusens are making this decree is because they have grown too weak and the guards of the peace have grown too powerful. They fear for their own lives and are making this decree to pacify the guards and attempt to keep themselves from being assassinated.

"Poor Emma!" exclaimed John, "She does not realize the danger that she herself is in! A man is a thousand times more likely to be killed in the city, and she lives in the capital city. The bell had tolled midnight. It was now the thirty-first. The Guards of the Peace were out to kill any that they thought were a threat to themselves or the Bergusens. The Society of the Independent Record-Keepers was viewed as a threat by Guards of the Peace. John Huntsford belonged to this society. Emma Myrtle was in no real danger. Had he forgotten that the Guards of the peace had outposts in the forests? The guards were out to kill him.



II.


That night John's sleep was disturbed with fleeting nightmares that left him up in bed, shaking with terror. His mind leaped back and forth between fear for himself and fear for Emma. Towards the morning, however; he began to calm down, and when he finally awoke he found that it was nearly ten o' clock.

"Oh dear!" he exclaimed to himself, "It's terribly late! No time for breakfast! I must be to the meeting at ten!"

"The meeting" was the weekly meeting of the Society of the independent Record-Keepers, who were dedicated to the preservation of honest and accurate records for future civilization. They realized that their own civilization was hovering in a drunken stagger over the dusty chute of histories incinerator and wished to leave to future generations a reliable record of their own existence and advice to help prevent future civilizations from making the same mistakes that theirs had. It was only natural, then, that the Bergusen royal family and the Guards of the Peace would help even out this force for good and be selfish to end, attempting, like so many other great empires, to give an inaccurate and exaggerated record of their own greatness and in an effort to set up an eternal boasting of themselves; should try to squelch any honest attempt at record-keeping. The Society of Independent Record-Keepers understood this; and knew that the Guards of the Peace would try to destroy their records, and so they kept all the original copies of their important records in a secret vault hidden deep in a cave in the heart of Mt. Kenly. The society was actually quite new, having been established only six years before by the then thirty-year old Henry Macer.

Mr. J. Huntsford, Third in authority of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers, walked into the house of Henry Macer, who was the president of the society. Meetings were held in his home, which was located in a small village of about 50 people and was the headquarters of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. The 334th weekly meeting of the society was about to begin, and it began with the usual routine.

"The mission and oath of the Society of independent Record Keepers." said Henry Macer, and the sixteen other active members of the society repeated with him: "To preserve an honest record of our civilization, to leave our posterity with a better future, and to press forward in the preservation of a clear conscience for ourselves and better moral values for mankind."

The formality of the meeting was slightly lowered by the presence of one of Mr. Macers two daughters, who was four years old, and sat in her fathers lap, quietly sucking her thumb. Mrs. Macer and her ten-year-old daughter had gone to the local grocers to buy some food; while the younger daughter had insisted to "stay with daddy." The meeting continued and the first agenda was presented. Nothing of great importance was to be discussed, only the presentation of two new members. Mr. Macer was quite well-to-do, and he lived in a large red two-story Victorian style house with over a dozen rooms and plenty of windows. In a village that consisted mainly of farmland and where the houses were huts, this house quite stood out. He was now reading off the last of the planned agenda. There had been little discussion; the agenda was simple, and they were ending early. The tone was one of contentment. Everything had been going as planned and they were quite happy about the proceedings. Mr. Macer had even said: "I wonder if there is still hope for our country. Perhaps it is not as bad as we think." John Huntsford hated to break the happy mood, but he knew that he had to say something. He decided to wait no longer.

"It is not as bad as you think." he stumbled out, "it is worse!"

The talking ceased. All men stared at John. "What is this you say?" said Mr. Macer.

"I have just received a letter." began John, and then paused for a little, "from Emma Myrtle. We are, as you know, engaged, or at least we plan to get married. Her parents are dead, and she lives with her aunt, who says that she will not allow her niece to marry a member of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers. When he had finished, everyone was silent. There was nothing to be said. The meeting was dismissed. Little Mary had left to go to her mother, who had come back from the store. John stayed behind to talk with Mr. Macer and Samuel Wilkins, President and Vice president of the Society of Independent Record-Keepers.

"I have to tell you something very important that has been weighing on my mind." said Mr. Macer. "It is not unlikely that one of us will be killed. It is a heavy one. The people that I would have in mind that I trust fully? I don't know. Ron Quely seems to be very trustworthy, but he has not been in the society for very long. Only choose those with simple honesty. If not, if we all die, than at least there will be a record, a vault, hidden deep in a mountain, that will one day be found. One day, after we are all gone and our civilization is forgotten and what remains is hopelessly distorted in oral tradition; and a new and greater civilization arises, when this civilization is faced with the question of monarchy or democracy, as ours once was, someone will find a record hidden in a mountain, and they will find it and preserve their own posterity from utter ruin. I am sure that they will make mistakes, very bad ones too, but they will have a record, a warning, given from a fallen civilization speaking from the dust, and they will establish a more perfect union. This is my hope, this is my dream." He stood silent and stared out the window for a while, saying nothing. John and Samuel Wilkins stood; lost in thought.

IV.


John loved to go on walks, and today was not an exception. He, for one, had never seen one Guard of the Peace except on a visit to the royal palace fifteen years ago as a child. His mother had been with him and they admired the beautiful artwork and furniture in the king's house. On the way out, he had asked what the darkly dressed man holding a sword was. His mother had answered with what, looking back, had not been a very pleasant tone of voice that they were the Guards of the Peace. Very little of the populace really liked the Guards of the Peace; and if one mentioned them to someone, they would always elicited a frown. John Huntsford thought pleasantly on his parents. VI.
